## Content Pillars

### 1. Product Showcases & Demos (30%)
Before/after transformations. Raw data turned into infographics. Template walkthroughs. "Watch this ugly spreadsheet become a stunning report in 12 seconds." This is the core content type — visual proof of the product's value.

### 2. Data Visualization Tips & Education (20%)
"5 rules for infographics that don't suck." Design principles for non-designers. Color theory basics. Chart type selection guides. Positions Wireframr as an authority on the subject it automates.

### 3. Use Case Spotlights (20%)
Marketing teams: campaign reports. Founders: pitch deck stats. Educators: lesson summaries. HR: onboarding infographics. Real estate: property comparison sheets. Sales: one-pagers. Each use case targets a different audience segment and demonstrates breadth.

### 4. Industry Commentary & Hot Takes (15%)
"Canva is for designers pretending to be marketers. Wireframr is for marketers who hate design." AI design tool opinions. Data visualization trends. State of infographics.

### 5. Build in Public / Behind the Scenes (15%)
Waitlist milestones ("Just hit 500 signups — here's what we learned"). Feature development peeks. User feedback reactions. Launch countdown. This creates narrative momentum and FOMO.

## Voice & Tone

### The Voice Formula

- **Formality:** Casual-professional. Like a smart friend explaining their cool side project.
- **Humor style:** Dry product humor. Self-aware startup humor. "We spent 3 weeks arguing about the perfect shade of blue for charts. Worth it."
- **Default energy:** Confident but not arrogant. Enthusiastic about the product without being salesy.
- **Sentence length:** Short and punchy for X. Medium for LinkedIn.
- **Vocabulary:** Simple, accessible. The audience is NOT designers — they're marketers, founders, educators who need infographics but hate making them.
- **Pronoun:** "We" (team voice). Never "I" (no single founder identity). Occasionally "Wireframr" in third person for feature announcements.

### DO:
- Show the product working (screen recordings, before/after, template previews)
- Give genuine data visualization advice even without mentioning Wireframr
- Use specific numbers ("1,047 templates" not "tons of templates")
- Acknowledge competitors honestly ("Canva is great for general design. We're laser-focused on infographics.")
- Create FOMO around the waitlist (milestone announcements, "spots filling up")
- Respond to everyone who engages — especially early on
- Use humor about the pain of making infographics manually

### DON'T:
- Sound like a corporate press release
- Use startup buzzwords ("disrupt," "revolutionize," "game-changer," "leverage," "synergy")
- Trash competitors directly — position against them by being different, not better
- Post feature lists without visual proof
- Be vague about what the product does
- Oversell before launch ("the best tool ever") — let the product visuals speak
- Use any word from the banned list in `_core-principles.md`
